Spirit Camper

  • Medium Game Design, Unreal Engine 5
  • Date November 2024
  • Video Embedded walkthrough below

A narrative-driven adventure game built in Unreal Engine 5. The player explores a forest, interacts with characters and objects, gathers materials, and helps Seraphina create a cure for a deadly poison.

Development Summary

This was my introduction to Unreal Engine 5 and digital game development. After exploring several ideas, I chose Spirit Camper for its narrative focus and built the project around exploration, dialogue, collectibles, and light environmental puzzles.

Design and Gameplay

I focused on player guidance and usability. Blocked paths, invisible walls, simple interface elements, and notes placed around the map helped guide players through the story clearly.

Technical and Creative Work

I sourced and arranged environmental assets such as trees, flowers, and a tombstone to shape the world and atmosphere. I also implemented teleportation, NPC interaction, and a key and door system, and used AI-generated voice lines for both characters.

Reflection

This project was challenging and rewarding because it was my first digital game in Unreal Engine 5. It taught me a lot about iteration, flexibility, and balancing scope with time. If I continued it, I would expand the story and add more mechanics.

Sheldon

  • Medium Scratch, Photoshop, Micro:bit, Programming, Clay Sculpture
  • Date December 2024
  • Video Embedded walkthrough below

Sheldon is a hybrid pet simulator that combines a clay turtle sculpture, micro:bit interaction, and a Scratch game. Players pet the turtle to raise its friendship meter, creating a simple and relaxing interactive experience.

Development Summary

My teammate and I created Sheldon as a hybrid project that joined physical and digital interaction. Inspired by a classmate's turtle-themed design, we explored how a sculpted object and a digital game could work together through touch input and Scratch gameplay.

My Role

I focused on the digital side by designing and coding the gameplay and menu in Scratch, building the friendship meter, writing Sheldon's dialogue, and shaping his reactions. I also sourced and edited AI-generated turtle images and used AI-generated voice lines for sound.

Physical and Digital Link

My partner sculpted the turtle in clay and used copper tape on the shell and belly to connect it to the micro:bit. That setup made the turtle responsive to touch and let the player's physical interaction directly affect the game.

Reflection

I am proud of how this project turned out. It brought together sculpture, programming, and interaction in a playful way, and the mix of physical object and digital interface helped Sheldon feel engaging and complete. With more time, I would expand the story and add more characters.

Vampire Slayer

  • Medium Electronics, Programming, Micro:bits, Cardboard
  • Date November 2024
  • Video Embedded walkthrough below

Vampire Slayer is a light-based interactive game where players use their phone flashlights to hit popping vampire heads. Inspired by Whack-a-Mole, the project combines micro:bits, motors, sensors, and a hand-built cardboard structure.

Development Summary

Our process began with brainstorming ideas and choosing the Whack-a-Mole format as the base for the project. We first prototyped the mechanics with styrofoam and a micro:bit motor, then refined the system into a seesaw mechanism that lifted and lowered the vampire heads.

How It Works

Players use their phone flashlights on the vampire heads as they appear. Each light sensor detects the flashlight and triggers the matching seesaw mechanism to lower the head back into the box.

My Contribution

I contributed materials for the build, including the large cardboard structure and coloured paper used for the game box. I also focused on the visual design, making sure the red and black vampire theme matched the mechanics and presentation.

Reflection

This project was a strong learning experience. My partner and I ran into challenges with the seesaw mechanism, but we improved it by adjusting the structure and movement system. Even with a few small issues, I was proud to see the idea work in physical form.

E.V.I.L.

  • Medium Board Game Design
  • Date March 2025
  • Format 3-player cooperative strategy game

E.V.I.L. is a cooperative strategy board game for three players where villains fight through branching paths of heroes using coordinated ability cards. The project combines worldbuilding, game systems, and visual design into a team-based experience focused on strategy and timing.

World and Concept

E.V.I.L., short for Every Villain Is Legendary, puts villains at the center of the story instead of the sidelines. Players work for the E.V.I.L. organization, move through hero territory, and rise through its ranks while fighting toward a final Legendary Hero.

Gameplay Summary

Players choose from Mutant, Witch, Cyborg, and Zombie, then manage small hands of ability cards across Strength, Magic, Tech, and Agility. Timing matters because players can save cards, combine powers, and trigger Ultimate Attacks by matching both cards.

Rules and Progression

Each villain starts with a 20-card ability deck and draws up to two cards at a time. Wins move the team forward, losses keep them in place, and Companion cards add one-use support effects after Super Hero battles. The team wins by defeating the Legendary Hero and loses if all players run out of cards.

My Role and Process

I led the concept development and MDA thinking, pitched the core idea, and helped shape the overall game flow. I also sketched characters, designed the final board map, supported the written materials, and helped adjust numbers and pacing through playtesting.

Reflection

This project taught me how to turn theme into mechanics while keeping scope under control. I enjoyed balancing systems, refining the rulebook, and building a cooperative game where teamwork and timing mattered.

The Heavy 20s

  • Medium Illustrator, Photoshop
  • Date November 2025
  • Format Individual magazine cover design

The Heavy 20s is a minimalist magazine cover designed for an article about anxiety in young adults. Inspired by Noma Bar's graphic style, I used bold typography and a simple visual metaphor to communicate two contrasting ideas within one editorial image.

Project Summary

This was an individual editorial design project based on a written article about anxiety, depression, and loneliness in young adulthood. I chose WIRED as the magazine because its focus on science, technology, and culture felt like a strong fit for the topic.

Concept Development

I began by identifying two contrasting words, carefree and burden, and sketching different visual ideas for each one. From there, I worked toward merging those ideas into a single element that could stay minimal while still carrying clear emotional meaning.

Process

After the sketch stage, I developed digital directions and tested multiple cover layouts, typography treatments, and colour variations. The final balloon-and-chain composition came out of that iteration process and helped communicate emotional weight in a direct, readable way.

Reflection

This was one of the most memorable projects for me because I spent a lot of time refining it before choosing the final version. It pushed me to think carefully about typography, cropping, colour contrast, spacing, and how a simple image can carry a complex idea.

Frottage

  • Medium Frottage Textures, Mixed Media
  • Date September 2024
  • Format Mixed media texture series

Frottage is a mixed media project that transforms texture rubbings from everyday surfaces into imaginative artworks. Using observation, pareidolia, and digital editing, I developed pieces that explore freedom, ascent, and unease.

Project Concept

This project began with an interest in frottage as a creative method. I was drawn to how familiar textures could reveal unexpected shapes and ideas, and I used those surfaces as starting points for storytelling rather than simple pattern-making.

Process

I collected rubbings from surfaces like sidewalks, tree bark, and shoe soles, then used pareidolia to find hidden forms within them. After that, I refined each composition in Photoshop to strengthen the final image and support its mood.

Artworks

Flight of the Free uses grounded textures to form a butterfly and suggest freedom. Heavenly Ascent turns textured shapes into a terraced staircase moving toward a dreamlike place. The Staring Shadows uses dark textures and red eyes to create tension and the feeling of being watched.

Reflection

This project showed me how textures can drive both creativity and storytelling. It pushed me to look past the original material and explore how observation, imagination, and digital editing can work together to create meaning.
